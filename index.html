Geothermal Energy Infographic Challenge

Goal - For the 2020 US Department of Energy Geothermal Design Challenge, me and two other students wanted to tackle the lack of public awareness around geothermal energies' potential and the technologies that are making broad geothermal energy possible.

Process - We decided to make an interactive infographic website from scratch. The whole process, from brainstorming to research to writing to art, design, & development took about two months. Doing the writing for the infographic was especially challenging because I was writing for a general audience so I needed to break down tricky topics into easily understood chunks. After writing and creating the artwork, we assembled the website in HTML & CSS. I used the Greensock animation library plus micromodal.js for it’s accessibility features and some custom interaction code to handle the rotating planet on all input types & responsive css & js to lay out all the elements on different form factors.

Result - Our site got 2nd place in the challenge & we hope it is reaching a broader audience thanks to the @geothermalfact twitter bot that a teammate wrote which tweets geothermal fun facts and directs people to our site.

Check out at the infographic at geothermal-future.web.app
— I hope you enjoy it and learn something too!

Web Graphics Experiments

Goal - For my final project of the computer graphics course at UCSC, I wanted to explore input methods & technologies beyond the polygonal raster graphics that power most real-time 3D rendering today. I decided to experiment with combining an efficient varient of ray tracing called ray marching combined with a gestural trackpad user interface.

Process - My first experiment tested the how the gestural user interface feels with the raw polygonal WebGL graphics pipeline we had been developing thoughout the course. After testing the feel of the gestures, I dived into learning about ray marching. Ray marching is an ingenious shortcut for efficiently rendering smooth shapes without any triangles. It accomplishes this by representing scenes as a continuous mathematical distance function that can be computed at each point in space. Then a GPU shader iteratively fires rays for each pixel into the distance function. Because the distance function encapsulates all objects in the scene, each ray can hone in on the nearest surface in few steps, and real-time reflections and illumination can be done with little overhead.

Result - As this was my first experience with ray marching and gesture interfaces on the web, it’s not as efficient or as intuitive as I would like, but I think the results are interesting and possibilities for ray marching are very real.

StudyHub 2

A web platform I designed and built for York School which allows students to share, organize, and access educational study resources. It's used by over 80% of York students and faculty to share and learn. Version 2 is built on Angular and uses Firebase, Algolia Search, and Google Cloud Functions based on Node JS as a backend. StudyHub has evolved continuously through many prototypes where I switched to new server stacks, made the web app responsive, mobile-ready and offline capable, and then did a major overhall of Studyhub after I asked two kind Google employees to review the security of my website. It took me nearly two years to complete the first version and I spend another six months totally reworking the codebase to be much faster and more feature-rich for version 2. You can play with an interactive prototype of the app or the live V2 site (requires york.org login) and learn more about StudyHub's development on my highschool student dev blog.

Seafloor Depth Data Visualization

I built this activity for the Seafloor Science and ROV Summer Camp that I work at over the summer. It's designed to teach kids how seafloor maps are made in the open ocean. It's a two-part project, the first being a robot fitted with a laser rangefinder, that "scans" a clay terrain model by driving on a glass sheet above the model and measuring distances. In the second part, camp participants paste the data they've collected into my website to see the model became a virtual seafloor map. This activity mimics how autonomous underwater vehicles and side-scanning boats create seafloor maps using sonar. It's built on WebGL using THREE.js and uses a custom GPU shader to render the depth-mapped color codes. You can play with it at kw-m.github.io/ssrov-heightmapper and look at the source code on my Github.
